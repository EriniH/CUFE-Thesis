You are free to use any font type and size within your figures and tables provided that they
are clear enough to the reader. For large size tables, you may separate them into several
pages. For large size figures that cannot be resized to fit an A4 size paper without the loss
of clarity, you may use larger size papers provided that they are properly folded to the A4
size and firmly bonded with the rest of the thesis.

\section{Location and Citation}
Figures and tables should be included in the main text as close to the point of their introduction as possible.

\section{Figures}
The document class defines a set of commands to insert figures and tables. For a centered, non-floating figure, you can use \verb|\gfigure{Caption}{Label}{Options}{File}|.
You can add a figure that takes the whole text width as follows. 
\gfigure{Text Width Figure Example}{fig1}{width=\linewidth}{Figures/Ch3/fig1.jpg}

Also, you can add a figure that takes a specific width as a ratio of the whole text width as follows. 
\gfigure{Half Text Width Figure Example}{fig2}{width=0.5\linewidth}{Figures/Ch3/fig2.jpg}


Also, you can add a figure with sub-figures as follows. 
\begin{figure}[!ht]
\centering
    \begin{subfigure}{0.3\textwidth}
    \centering    
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Figures/Ch3/fig2.jpg}
    \caption{Sub-Figure 1 Caption}
    \figlbl{subfig1} 
    \end{subfigure}
\hspace{0.5in}%horizontal space between figures
    \begin{subfigure}{0.3\textwidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Figures/Ch3/fig2.jpg}
    \caption{Sub-Figure 2 Caption}
    \figlbl{subfig2}
    \end{subfigure}
\caption{Figure With two Sub-Figures Example}
\figlbl{fig3}
\end{figure}


You can make a rotated figure as follows:
\begin{sidewaysfigure}[!ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{Figures/Ch3/fig1.jpg}
\caption{Rotated Figure Example}
\figlbl{rotatedfig}
\end{sidewaysfigure}
\newpage
The document class also defines set of commands for labels and references. You can label a figure using \verb|\figlbl{Label}| and refer to it using \verb|\figref{Label}|. For example, \figref{fig1} is an example of a figure that takes the whole text width. you can also refer to the sub-figures as follows: \figref{subfig1} and \figref{subfig2}.

\section{Tables}

The document class also defines \verb|hctable| enviroment for centered, non-floating tables. You can create tables as follows:

\begin{hctable}{Table Example}{tab1}
%% Some packages, such as MDW tools, booktabs, tabularx offer better commands for making tables
%% than the plain LaTeX2e tabular which is used here.
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
\begin{tabular}{c || c | c | c}
\hline
\hline
A & B & C & D\\
\hline
\hline
A & B & C & D\\
\hline
A & B & C & D\\
\hline
A & B & C & D\\
\hline
A & B & C & D\\
\hline
\multicolumn{4}{c}{Multi Column option }\\
\hline
A & B & \multicolumn{2}{c}{Multi Column option }\\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{hctable}


Also you can create a rotated table for comparison, for example:

\begin{sidewaystable}[!ht]
\begin{center}
\begin{threeparttable}
\caption{Rotated Comparison Table Example}
\tablbl{comprotated}
\centering
\begin{tabular}{c || c | c | c | c | c | c}
\hline\hline
Parameters  & Ref1  & Ref2  & Ref3  & Ref4  & Ref5  & \textbf{This*}\\
\hline\hline
Year**      & 2010  & 2008  & 2016  & 2006  & 2019  & \textbf{2019} \\
\hline
Param1      & 2010  & 2008  & 2016  & 2006  & 2019  & \textbf{2019} \\
\hline
Param2      & 2010  & 2008  & 2016  & 2006  & 2019  & \textbf{2019} \\
\hline
Param3***   & 2010  & 2008  & 2016  & 2006  & 2019  & \textbf{2019} \\
\hline
Param4      & 2010  & 2008  & 2016  & 2006  & 2019  & \textbf{2019} \\
\hline\hline
\end{tabular}
	\begin{tablenotes}
		\small
		\centering
		\item *: Note 1
		\item **: Note 2
		\item ***: Note 3
	\end{tablenotes}
\end{threeparttable}
\end{center}
\end{sidewaystable}

Like figures, you can label tables using \verb|\tablbl{Label}| and refer to them using \verb|\tabref{Label}|. For example, \tabref{comprotated} is an example of a rotated comparison table.

\section{Equations}
You can write an equation with a label using \verb|\eqnlbl{Label}| and refer to it using \verb|\eqnref{Label}| as \eqnref{divergenceB} as follows:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\nabla} \cdot \mathbf{B} = \mathbf{0}
    \eqnlbl{divergenceB}
\end{equation}

\section{Other Cross-References}
you can also label and refer to chapters, sections, and subsections using \verb|\chlbl{Label}| and \verb|\chref{Label}| for chapters, \verb|\seclbl{Label}| and \verb|\secref{Label}| for sections. For example, \secref{sec_conc} is an example of a section reference inside \chref{conc}. The command \verb|\chlbl{Label}| is not much useful as the chapter title is already included in the chapter command \verb|Chapter[Label]{Chapter Heading}{Path to Chapter File}|, but it is provided for consistency with other labels and references. You can also label and refer to appendices using \verb|\applbl{Label}| and \verb|\appref{Label}|, respectively. Likewise, \verb|\applbl{Label}| is not much useful as the appendix title is already included in the appendix command \verb|\Appendix[Label]{Appendix Heading}{Path to Appendix File}|.
